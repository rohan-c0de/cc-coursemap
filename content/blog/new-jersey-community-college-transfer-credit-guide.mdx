# How New Jersey Community College Transfer Credit Actually Works: An NJTransfer Student's Guide

New Jersey has 18 county colleges and over 40 four-year institutions that accept their credits — but the same community college course can be a direct match at Rowan, worth nothing at Rutgers Engineering, and somewhere in between at Seton Hall. NJTransfer.org publishes these outcomes, but the sheer volume of data makes it hard to see the patterns that matter most.

We analyzed tens of thousands of published transfer equivalencies from NJTransfer.org across all 40 receiving institutions. The results reveal a transfer landscape where your choice of destination university matters as much as your choice of courses — and where some paths are dramatically better than others.

## How NJTransfer works

NJTransfer.org is New Jersey's official transfer equivalency system. For each community college course, it publishes how every participating four-year institution evaluates that course. The outcomes are binary:

1. **Direct match.** Your community college course maps to a specific university course. If that university course satisfies a gen-ed or major requirement, yours does too.
2. **No credit.** The university does not accept the course for any credit at all.

Unlike some states that have a middle category of "elective credit" (where you get hours but they don't count toward specific requirements), NJTransfer's published equivalencies tend to be all-or-nothing. A course either maps to something specific or it doesn't transfer.

This makes New Jersey's system unusually transparent — but also unusually high-stakes. There's no soft landing of elective credit. If your course doesn't have a direct match, you may get nothing.

## The universities: from 96% acceptance to 13%

The 40 four-year institutions on NJTransfer accept community college courses at wildly different rates. The spread is the largest we've seen in any state.

### Tier 1: Transfer-friendly (80%+ direct match rate)

These universities accept the vast majority of community college coursework as direct equivalencies:

- **Rowan University** — 96% direct match rate
- **William Paterson University** — 95%
- **Rutgers School of Management & Labor Relations** — 94%
- **Caldwell University** — 94%
- **Thomas Edison State University** — 93%
- **Rutgers Bloustein School of Planning** — 93%
- **Centenary University** — 91%
- **Georgian Court University** — 90%
- **Felician University** — 84%
- **Kean University** — 81%

At these schools, the default is acceptance. If you take a standard community college course, it will almost certainly transfer as a direct match. Rowan and William Paterson are especially strong — fewer than 5% of evaluated courses receive no credit.

### Tier 2: Selective (50%–80% direct match rate)

The majority of NJ's four-year schools fall in this range:

- **Rutgers School of Environmental & Biological Sciences** — 74%
- **Montclair State University (Bloomfield College)** — 73%
- **Rutgers Camden** — 72%
- **Rutgers Newark** (multiple schools) — 70–72%
- **Fairleigh Dickinson** (both campuses) — 69%
- **New Jersey City University** — 66%
- **Saint Elizabeth University** — 64%
- **The College of New Jersey (TCNJ)** — 63%
- **Monmouth University** — 63%
- **Ramapo College** — 58%
- **Drew University** — 55%
- **Saint Peter's University** — 54%
- **Stockton University** — 53%
- **Rutgers School of Nursing** — 51%
- **Rider University** — 51%
- **Seton Hall University** — 50%

At these schools, course selection matters. About half to three-quarters of your community college work will transfer directly, but a meaningful share won't. Checking before you register is essential.

### Tier 3: Highly selective (under 40% direct match rate)

These institutions accept a minority of community college courses as direct matches:

- **Rutgers Mason Gross School of the Arts** — 39%
- **Rutgers Business School (New Brunswick)** — 39%
- **Rutgers School of Arts & Sciences** — 36%
- **DeVry University** — 33%
- **Berkeley College** — 29%
- **New Jersey Institute of Technology (NJIT)** — 21%
- **Rutgers Ernest Mario School of Pharmacy** — 18%
- **Rutgers School of Engineering** — 13%

The bottom four are striking. At NJIT, roughly four out of five community college courses receive no credit. At Rutgers Engineering, it's nearly seven out of eight. These aren't bad schools — they're specialized programs with narrow equivalency definitions. But if you're targeting them, you need to be extremely deliberate about which courses you take at the community college level.

## The Rutgers puzzle: one university, ten different answers

Rutgers is technically one university, but for transfer purposes it operates as roughly a dozen separate entities. The same community college course can have completely different outcomes depending on which Rutgers school you're applying to:

- **Rutgers School of Management & Labor Relations** — 94% direct
- **Rutgers Bloustein School of Planning** — 93% direct
- **Rutgers School of Environmental & Biological Sciences** — 74% direct
- **Rutgers Camden** — 72% direct
- **Rutgers Newark** — 70% direct
- **Rutgers Mason Gross School of the Arts** — 39% direct
- **Rutgers Business School (New Brunswick)** — 39% direct
- **Rutgers School of Arts & Sciences** — 36% direct
- **Rutgers School of Nursing** — 51% direct
- **Rutgers Ernest Mario School of Pharmacy** — 18% direct
- **Rutgers School of Engineering** — 13% direct

The spread from 94% to 13% within one university system is remarkable. A student targeting Rutgers who doesn't specify which school could face outcomes that are seven times better or worse depending on the program. "I'm transferring to Rutgers" is not a meaningful statement without knowing which Rutgers.

## Courses that transfer everywhere

Despite the variation across universities, some community college courses are direct matches at all 40 institutions in the NJTransfer system. These include:

- **Accounting** — Principles of Accounting I and II, Computerized Accounting
- **Anthropology** — Introduction to Anthropology, Cultural Anthropology, Biological Anthropology
- **Art** — Art Appreciation, Art History I, Drawing, Three-Dimensional Design
- **Arabic** — Elementary Arabic I and II
- **American Sign Language** — ASL I and II
- **Astronomy** — Introduction to Astronomy

The pattern is consistent with what we see across other states: introductory liberal arts and social science courses have the widest portability. If you're unsure where you'll transfer, these subjects are the safest foundation.

## Courses that don't transfer anywhere

At the other extreme, some courses are rejected by every university that evaluates them. In NJTransfer's data, these are exclusively developmental and remedial courses:

- **Study Skills** courses (verbal development, fundamentals of writing, reading, and computation)
- **Adult Literacy Preparation** courses
- **Academic Skills** courses

This isn't a surprise — developmental courses aren't designed for transfer credit. But students should be aware: if your community college places you in developmental courses, those credits will never transfer. They're a necessary bridge to college-level work, not transferable currency.

Beyond developmental courses, **vocational and technical subjects** have the highest no-credit rates. Automotive technology, aviation technology, and similar applied programs lose credit at most four-year institutions. These courses serve their programs well, but they're designed for workforce entry, not university transfer.

## How to plan your courses for transfer

### 1. Know which school — and which program — you're targeting

The difference between Rowan (96% direct) and NJIT (21% direct) is not subtle. And within Rutgers, the difference between Management (94%) and Engineering (13%) is even more dramatic. "I want to go to a four-year school" is not specific enough for good course planning in New Jersey.

### 2. Check NJTransfer before every registration

NJTransfer.org tells you exactly how your course will be evaluated at your target institution. Before you register, look up every course. Don't assume that because a course transferred for a friend at Rowan, it'll transfer at Rutgers SAS.

### 3. If you're targeting a Tier 3 school, plan backwards

For NJIT, Rutgers Engineering, Rutgers Pharmacy, or Rutgers Business (New Brunswick), you should start with the university's accepted equivalencies and build your community college schedule from that list. Don't take courses hoping they'll transfer — take courses you've confirmed will transfer.

### 4. Don't confuse "credit" with "useful credit"

New Jersey's system is binary — direct match or no credit. But even a direct match might not help if it doesn't satisfy a requirement in your specific degree program. Check not just whether your course transfers, but whether the university course it maps to fulfills a requirement you need.

### 5. Get a preliminary credit evaluation before committing

Most NJ four-year schools will do a preliminary transfer credit evaluation before you formally apply. Take advantage of this. Bring your transcript and your target degree plan, and ask specifically which courses will apply to your program requirements.

## Common mistakes NJ transfer students make

### Assuming all Rutgers schools are the same

They aren't. The same course can be a direct match at Rutgers Management and receive no credit at Rutgers Engineering. Always check the specific Rutgers school, not just "Rutgers."

### Not checking before registering

NJTransfer exists precisely so you can check before you commit to a course. Every semester, students discover after the fact that their courses didn't transfer. The tool is free and takes minutes to use.

### Targeting a Tier 3 school without adjusting course selection

If you're aiming for NJIT or a specialized Rutgers school, you cannot take a standard community college curriculum and expect most of it to transfer. You need to plan around the university's specific equivalencies from day one.

### Taking vocational courses expecting university credit

Automotive technology, aviation, and similar vocational courses serve clear career purposes — but they don't transfer to four-year institutions. If your goal is transfer, these courses won't help you get there.

### Waiting until the transfer application to discover problems

By then you've spent semesters and money on courses that may not serve your goal. The time to check is before your first community college registration, not when you're filling out the transfer application.

## The bottom line

New Jersey's transfer landscape is the most variable we've seen across any state. The same community college course can have 40 different outcomes — from automatic acceptance to zero credit — depending entirely on where you're headed. Universities like Rowan and William Paterson accept nearly everything. Rutgers Engineering and NJIT accept almost nothing from community colleges.

NJTransfer.org publishes all of this data. The students who transfer efficiently are the ones who use it before every registration, target specific universities and programs rather than vague goals, and understand that in New Jersey's binary system, there's no middle ground between "this counts" and "this doesn't."
