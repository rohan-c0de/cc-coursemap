# CC CourseMap Blog Article Generation Brief

## Purpose
Use this file when creating blog articles for CC CourseMap.

The goal is **not** to create generic student-advice blog posts.

The goal is to create **meaningful, SEO-worthy, product-aligned articles** that attract the right visitors and naturally connect to CC CourseMap’s actual utility:
- searching community college courses
- transfer planning
- schedule-building
- senior tuition-waiver / auditing discovery
- reducing wasted credits, confusion, and decision friction

CC CourseMap currently helps users across multiple states, including Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and DC.

---

## Core editorial philosophy
Every article should do at least one of these well:
- help a reader make a better decision
- help a reader avoid a costly mistake
- save a reader time
- save a reader money
- reduce confusion or anxiety
- make the product more obviously useful

Do **not** write fluffy content.
Do **not** write broad education-blog filler.
Do **not** write “tips” content unless it is tightly tied to a real product-relevant decision.

---

## What kinds of articles to create
Prioritize articles around these themes:

### 1. Transfer confusion and “will this actually count?”
Examples:
- direct match vs elective credit
- how to tell whether a course will count
- how transfer rules differ by state/system
- how a course can transfer and still not help you graduate

### 2. Registration timing and course availability
Examples:
- late-start classes
- when schedules go live
- what to do when the class you need is full
- how to find alternatives fast

### 3. Cross-college schedule building
Examples:
- taking classes at more than one community college
- finding online/evening/weekend options
- building a workable schedule across campuses or colleges

### 4. Senior tuition waivers, auditing, and low-cost access
Examples:
- free or reduced-cost classes for seniors
- audit vs credit
- state-specific senior rules
- practical how-it-works guides

### 5. State-system explainers
Examples:
- Virginia transfer pathways
- North Carolina transfer agreements
- DC-specific transfer credit questions
- rules and friction unique to a specific state system

### 6. Mistake-avoidance content
Examples:
- common transfer mistakes
- misunderstanding elective credit
- assuming availability matters more than transfer value
- hidden traps in course planning

## State-specific vs general article rules
For each article idea, explicitly decide whether it should be:

- **general**
- **state-specific**
- **general + state-specific cluster**

Use **general** when:
- the confusion is conceptual across states
- the article explains a core concept
- the content builds category understanding

Use **state-specific** when:
- rules differ materially by state
- transfer pathways differ by state/system
- senior waiver/audit rules differ by state
- the search intent is clearly location/system-bound

Use **general + state-specific cluster** when:
- the broad concept deserves one strong explainer
- but real user decisions depend on state-specific details

Examples:
- “What direct match vs elective credit means” = general
- “Virginia community college transfer: how guaranteed admission works” = state-specific
- “Free or reduced-cost community college classes for seniors” = general hub
- “Virginia senior tuition waiver rules explained” = state-specific spoke

---

## Content quality rules
Every article must be:
- specific
- useful
- concrete
- honest
- product-aligned

Avoid:
- “Top 10 tips” framing
- broad motivational education content
- weakly related student lifestyle content
- articles that attract lots of traffic but low-quality visitors
- content that sounds like an admissions blog
- content that overpromises certainty

Prefer:
- decision support
- comparison
- explanation of confusing concepts
- process clarity
- state/system-specific interpretation
- practical examples
- scenario-driven writing

---

## SEO and strategic rules
When selecting or writing an article, think beyond clicks.

Consider:
- Is the search intent high quality?
- Is this visitor likely to use CC CourseMap?
- Does this topic naturally lead to course search, transfer lookup, schedule building, or senior waiver discovery?
- Could this article build trust and authority over time?
- Could this article earn backlinks from advisors, parents, nonprofits, schools, or community forums?
- Is this topic too broad, too competitive, or too generic?
- Is this topic durable and evergreen?
- **Does this topic have real search volume?** Before writing, do a quick keyword validation. A great article that nobody searches for is a wasted effort. Prioritize topics where people are actively looking for answers.

Do **not** optimize for vanity traffic.

---

## Preferred article structure
Unless there is a strong reason not to, structure each article like this:

1. **Clear headline**
2. **Sharp intro** that frames the real problem in plain English
3. **Why this is confusing / what people get wrong**
4. **Practical explanation**
5. **Examples or scenarios**
6. **State-specific nuance**, if relevant
7. **How CC CourseMap helps**, naturally and non-spammy
8. **Conclusion / next step**

The article should feel like it was written by someone who understands the problem deeply, not by a generic content writer.

---

## Article length
Match depth to the topic. Do not pad.

- A focused explainer (e.g., "What does audit mean?") might be 600–900 words.
- A comprehensive guide (e.g., "How Virginia transfer works") might be 1,500–2,000 words.
- Never write long for the sake of length. If the topic is fully covered in 800 words, stop at 800 words.

The goal is density, not volume.

---

## Internal linking rules
Every article should link to other content on the site. This helps SEO, keeps readers engaged, and makes the product more discoverable.

**Article-to-tool links:**
- Each article should include at least 1 natural link to a CC CourseMap tool page (course search, transfer lookup, schedule builder, starting soon, etc.)
- Use the `<ProductCallout>` component for prominent in-article CTAs. Use regular markdown links for lighter inline mentions.

**Article-to-article links:**
- Each article should link to 2–3 other related articles where the reference is genuinely useful.
- Hub articles should link to all their spoke articles.
- Spoke articles should link back to their hub and to at least one sibling spoke.

**Avoid:**
- Linking to unrelated articles just to increase link count
- Stuffing links into the intro or conclusion where they feel forced
- Linking the same article more than once in the same post

---

## Content freshness and review cadence
Some articles are evergreen. Others will go stale.

**Flag articles for annual review when they contain:**
- State-specific rules (senior waiver ages, eligibility criteria, legal citations)
- Transfer agreement details (CAA, GAA, specific equivalency tables)
- Specific process steps that depend on college or state system policies
- Fee amounts or cost details

**Evergreen articles that rarely need updates:**
- Conceptual explainers (what is elective credit, what does auditing mean)
- General decision frameworks (how to evaluate transfer value)
- Mistake-avoidance content (unless the mistakes change)

When writing an article, note in the metadata whether it needs periodic review. This prevents stale content from eroding trust.

---

## Tone rules
Write like:
- a sharp product-aware guide
- a practical expert
- someone helping a real person avoid a mistake

Do not write like:
- a generic SEO freelancer
- a vague “education consultant”
- a marketer stuffing keywords
- a hypey startup voice

Tone should be:
- calm
- specific
- helpful
- credible
- lightly authoritative
- not fluffy

---

## Product-integration rules
Each article should connect naturally to the product.

That does **not** mean forced sales language.

Instead:
- show how the problem connects to course search, transfer lookup, schedule planning, or waiver discovery
- reference product use only where genuinely helpful
- keep the product mention practical, not promotional

Bad:
- “Use CC CourseMap today for all your education needs!”

Good:
- “If you are comparing multiple community colleges or trying to see what is available now, a tool like CC CourseMap can reduce that back-and-forth.”

---

## Article types that may be better as non-blog content
If a requested topic is better as something else, say so.

Some topics may be better as:
- programmatic pages
- data pages
- glossary pages
- comparison pages
- state landing pages
- tool-like pages

Do not force everything into blog format.

## High-priority article ideas to start from
Use these as preferred starting points. They are grouped into clusters where overlap exists to avoid cannibalizing search intent across separate posts.

### Cluster A: Transfer credit confusion (hub + spokes)
- **Hub:** Community College Transfer Credit: What “Direct Match” vs “Elective Credit” Actually Means
  - This is the comprehensive conceptual piece. Fold in “A Course Can Transfer and Still Not Help You Graduate” and “Community College Transfer Mistakes” as sections within this article rather than writing them as separate posts. They address the same core confusion.
- **Spoke (VA):** Virginia Community College Transfer: How Guaranteed Admission Actually Works
- **Spoke (NC):** North Carolina Community College to UNC Transfer: What the CAA Covers and What It Does Not
- **Spoke (NC):** North Carolina Transfer Guides: How to Use Them Without Getting Lost

### Cluster B: Senior waivers and auditing (hub + spokes)
- **Hub:** Free or Reduced-Cost Community College Classes for Seniors: Which States Actually Offer Them?
- **Spoke (VA):** Virginia Senior Citizens and Community College: Free Tuition vs Audit-Only Explained
- **Spoke (NC):** North Carolina Senior Citizens at Community Colleges: What 65+ Students Can Still Do
- **Standalone (general):** What Does “Audit a Class” Actually Mean at a Community College?

### Standalone articles (no cluster needed)
- How to Tell if a Community College Course Will Count for Your Target University Before You Enroll
- How to Find Late-Start Community College Classes Before the Semester Is Lost
- Can You Take Classes at More Than One Community College at the Same Time?
- How to Build a Community College Schedule Across Multiple Campuses or Colleges
- When Do Community College Class Schedules Usually Go Live?
  - *Note: Validate search volume before writing. This may have low demand and could be better as a short FAQ entry on the About page.*

### Recommended writing order
Write hub articles first, then spokes. Hubs build the conceptual foundation that spokes link back to.

1. Cluster A hub (direct match vs elective credit)
2. What does “audit a class” mean (standalone, foundational)
3. Cluster B hub (senior waivers overview)
4. Late-start classes (standalone, high product alignment with Starting Soon)
5. Cluster A spokes (VA transfer, NC transfer)
6. Cluster B spokes (VA seniors, NC seniors)
7. Remaining standalone articles

## What to do when asked to create an article
When asked to write an article, follow this workflow:

### Step 1: Validate the article type
Before drafting, determine:
- Is this article general, state-specific, or both?
- Is this actually best as a blog post, or would another format be better?

### Step 2: State the strategic framing
Before writing, briefly state:
- target reader
- search intent
- why this topic is worth publishing
- how it connects to CC CourseMap

### Step 3: Write the article
Write the full draft with:
- meaningful substance
- no fluff
- practical examples
- clean structure
- natural product alignment

### Step 4: End with a practical next step
The ending should help the reader move forward.
It can point them to a useful action, not just a generic summary.

---

## Output expectations for article generation
When generating an article, produce:

1. **Recommended title**
2. **Article type**: general / state-specific / cluster (hub or spoke)
3. **Target reader**
4. **Search intent**
5. **Why this is strategically good**
6. **Needs periodic review?** Yes/no, and why
7. **Full draft** (with internal links to tool pages and related articles woven in naturally)
8. **Suggested internal link opportunities** (both tool pages and other articles)
9. **If relevant, suggested follow-up companion articles**
